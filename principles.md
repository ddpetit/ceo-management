# Operating Principles

These are the beliefs that shape how this system works — and how you might approach your own operating rhythm.

Read them once. Return when you feel lost.

---

## On Clarity

**Clarity is the ultimate competitive advantage.**

Most executives don't lack intelligence, resources, or opportunities. They lack clarity — about what they want, what matters, and what to do next. This system exists to create and maintain clarity.

Clarity compounds. A clear week builds on a clear day. A clear quarter builds on clear weeks. A clear decade builds on clear years. The work is staying clear long enough for the compounding to matter.

---

## On Reflection

**Reflection is not a luxury. It is maintenance.**

You wouldn't run a company without looking at the numbers. Don't run a life without looking at the patterns. Regular reflection is how you catch drift before it becomes crisis.

Five minutes daily. Thirty minutes weekly. An hour quarterly. A half-day annually. This is not overhead — it's the minimum investment in knowing where you actually are.

---

## On Honesty

**This system only works if you're honest with yourself.**

No one sees these files but you. There's no performance to maintain, no narrative to protect, no reputation to manage. The only person you can lie to here is yourself.

When you notice yourself softening language, avoiding topics, or spinning failures — stop. That's exactly where the insight lives.

---

## On Energy

**Manage energy, not just time.**

Time management is a solved problem. Energy management is not. You can have eight hours available and accomplish nothing because you're depleted. You can have two hours available and move mountains because you're resourced.

Track your energy. Notice what fills it. Notice what drains it. Protect what fills. Eliminate or delegate what drains.

---

## On Patterns

**Look for what repeats.**

A single bad week is noise. The same bad pattern for three years is signal. This system emphasizes pattern recognition because patterns reveal what's actually true — not what you hope is true or what you tell others is true.

Your past reviews, your repeated goals, your chronic failures — they contain information. Extract it.

---

## On Simplicity

**If it's too complicated, you won't do it.**

The best system is the one you actually use. Elaborate systems fail not because they're wrong but because they're heavy. This system is designed to be light enough to maintain indefinitely.

If any part feels like bureaucracy, simplify it or skip it. The goal is insight, not compliance.

---

## On Decisions

**Most decisions are reversible. Act accordingly.**

Agonizing over reversible decisions wastes time and energy. Make them quickly, learn from the results, adjust. Save your careful deliberation for the truly irreversible ones: major hires, major investments, major commitments.

The cost of indecision usually exceeds the cost of a wrong decision.

---

## On Goals

**Goals are hypotheses, not commitments.**

A goal is your best guess about what future state would make you satisfied. It's not a blood oath. When you learn new information — about yourself, your circumstances, your desires — update the goal.

Stubbornly pursuing outdated goals is not discipline. It's denial.

---

## On Time Horizons

**Think in decades. Plan in years. Work in days.**

Long-term thinking creates context. Annual planning creates direction. Daily action creates results. All three are necessary. None is sufficient alone.

The daily question: "Does this move me toward my decade goals?"
The annual question: "Am I still pursuing the right decade goals?"

---

## On Leverage

**Not all effort is equal.**

Some actions produce 10x the results of others with the same effort. Finding and exploiting these leverage points is the primary job of an executive.

Before adding anything to your plate, ask: "What's the leverage here?" If the answer isn't clear, question whether it belongs on your plate at all.

---

## On Regret

**Minimize regret, not risk.**

Jeff Bezos's regret minimization framework: Project yourself to age 80 and ask which choice you'd regret more — trying and failing, or never trying. Most people overweight the risk of action and underweight the regret of inaction.

When in doubt, bias toward action on things that matter to you.

---

## On Enough

**Define what enough looks like.**

Without a clear definition of "enough," you'll optimize forever. Enough money. Enough status. Enough achievement. Enough impact. If you don't define it, you'll never reach it — and you'll sacrifice everything else in the pursuit.

Write down your "enough." Revisit it annually. Notice if it keeps moving.

---

## On Seasons

**Life has seasons. Respect them.**

There are seasons for building and seasons for maintaining. Seasons for sprinting and seasons for recovering. Seasons for expanding and seasons for contracting. Fighting your current season creates suffering.

Name your season. Work with it, not against it.

---

## On the System Itself

**This system serves you. You don't serve it.**

If a framework doesn't resonate, skip it. If a prompt doesn't land, rewrite it. If a rhythm doesn't fit, change it. The goal is clarity and progress, not adherence to someone else's structure.

Use what works. Discard what doesn't. Make it yours.
